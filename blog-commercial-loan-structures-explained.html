Commercial Loan Structures Explained for Business Owners

Term loans, revolving credit facilities, asset finance — which structure fits your cash-flow cycle and growth plans?

← Back to all articles
CommercialMay 15, 20267 min read

Choosing the right commercial loan structure can mean the difference between smooth cash flow and constant repayment pressure. Each option serves a different purpose, and matching the structure to your business cycle is critical.

Term Loans

A term loan provides a lump sum upfront, repaid over a fixed period with regular principal-and-interest or interest-only payments. Best suited for one-off investments such as purchasing commercial property, funding a major expansion, or acquiring another business.

Key features

  • Predictable repayments on a set schedule
  • Typically 1–20 year terms depending on purpose
  • Secured against business assets or property
  • Early repayment may attract break costs

Revolving Credit Facilities (RCF)

An RCF gives you access to a credit limit that you can draw from, repay, and redraw as needed. It works like a large business overdraft and is ideal for managing working capital, seasonal cash-flow gaps, or ongoing operational expenses.

Key features

  • Pay interest only on the amount drawn
  • Flexible access to funds within your limit
  • Annual review rather than fixed term
  • Facility fees apply regardless of usage

Asset Finance

Asset finance lets you borrow specifically to purchase equipment, vehicles, or machinery. The asset itself secures the loan, which often means better rates and longer terms than unsecured options.

Types of asset finance

  • Hire purchase: You own the asset at the end of the term; repayments include principal and interest.
  • Commercial lease: The lender buys the asset and leases it to you; flexible end-of-term options.
  • Finance lease: Similar to hire purchase but the lender holds legal title until final payment.

Matching Structure to Purpose

The right choice depends on your cash-flow pattern, tax position, and growth plans. A trading business with seasonal revenue may benefit from an RCF, while a manufacturer buying new equipment should look at asset finance.
