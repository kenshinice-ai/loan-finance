First Home Buyer Guide: What Changed in 2026

Deposit thresholds, government schemes, and lender policy shifts every first-time buyer should know before making an offer.

← Back to all articles
Home Loans June 15, 2026 8 min read

The Australian housing market continues to evolve in 2026, and first-home buyers face a different landscape than just two years ago. Deposit requirements have shifted in several states, government guarantee programs have expanded, and lender appetite for low-deposit lending varies significantly across the panel.

Deposit Requirements: What You Need Now

The traditional 20% deposit benchmark has given way to more flexible pathways. Many lenders now accept deposits as low as 5%, though LMI (Lenders Mortgage Insurance) remains a factor. In Melbourne and Sydney, average first-home prices sit above $600,000, meaning even a 5% deposit requires roughly $30,000–$40,000 in savings.

Government Schemes That Help

  • The First Home Guarantee (FHG): Allows eligible first-home buyers to purchase with a 5% deposit without LMI. The federal program has expanded capacity for 2026.
  • State-based stamp duty concessions: Victoria, Queensland, and Western Australia all offer reduced or exempted stamp duty for first-home purchases under certain price thresholds.
  • First Home Loan Deposit Scheme extensions: The federal government has extended the scheme to help eligible buyers avoid LMI on smaller deposits.

Lender Policy Shifts

Several major banks have tightened serviceability buffers in response to rising living costs, while some non-bank lenders have become more competitive on pricing for first-home buyers. The key is comparing the total cost of borrowing — not just the headline rate.

What to check before you apply

  1. Your deposit size and whether LMI applies
  2. Eligibility for government schemes in your state
  3. Your borrowing capacity based on current income and expenses
  4. The property type the lender accepts (some restrict on investment or off-plan purchases)

Timing Your Purchase

Market conditions in 2026 suggest a balanced approach. Interest rates have stabilised compared to the peak cycle, but supply constraints in key corridors mean prices remain firm. First-home buyers who enter the market with pre-approval and clear criteria are well-positioned.
